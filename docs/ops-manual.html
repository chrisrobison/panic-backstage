How to Use This Manual

Two books, one system

Think of Panic Backstage as having two guides: the Reference (the ? help icons inside the app that explain every field) and this — the Learning book that walks you through whole workflows from beginning to end.

When you're staring at a field you don't recognize, click the ? link next to the section heading to open the in-app reference for that area. When you want to understand why something works the way it does, or how all the pieces fit together across a show's lifecycle, that's what this manual is for.

A note on Panic Backstage

Panic Backstage is the internal management system for your venue. It tracks every show from the moment someone asks "are you free on the 14th?" all the way through settlement and closing the books. It's the single source of truth — what's in Backstage is what's real.

Staff access Backstage through a web browser. Most things you'd want to do during a show (check in guests, scan tickets, look up the run sheet) work fine on a phone.

📋 The Three Workflows

This manual covers the three major workflows in order:

Chapter 1 — Booking: From the first inquiry call to the contract signed and date locked.

Chapter 2 — Run of Night: Everything that happens the day of the show, from load-in to last call.

Chapter 3 — Post-Event: Settlement, archiving, and closing the books.

Part I

Lead Pipeline

Chapter 1

Lead Pipeline

Most bookings don't start as events — they start as leads. The Lead Pipeline is where you evaluate inquiries, run the numbers, and decide what to book before anything hits the calendar.

The Leads Inbox

Before an event is created, most bookings start as a lead — an inquiry or opportunity that needs evaluation. The Leads Inbox (sidebar nav → Leads) shows all leads in a pipeline view.

The inbox table shows the following columns:

Status filter tabs across the top let you narrow the view: All | New | Triage | Evaluating | Needs Review | Approved.

Needs Review badge

A Needs Review badge appears on the sidebar Leads link when leads are stalled and need manager attention. The dashboard card Leads Needing Review shows the count for venue admins so nothing slips through.

Lead sources

Each lead has a source that tracks where the inquiry originated. Available sources: Website, Promoter, Peerspace, Eventective, Giggster, Phone, Email, Manual. This data is preserved for reporting — over time you can see which channels generate the most bookable leads.

Declining vs. canceling

Use Declined when you've actively decided not to book the inquiry — you had the information and said no. The Deal Evaluator

On the lead detail panel, the right column is the Deal Evaluator — a financial projection tool that tells you whether a show is worth booking before you commit to it.

Projection fields

Deal Evaluator Inputs
Room capacity
The hard legal limit for the room. Used to flag if attendance assumptions exceed the room.
Expected attendance
Your projected headcount. The key variable — small changes here move the numbers significantly.
Ticket price
Advance ticket price. Used to calculate gross ticket revenue and break-even.
Ticket fee per ticket
Service/processing fee charged on top of the ticket price (if any). Added to ticket revenue.
Rental fee
For private events — the flat venue rental amount.
Artist guarantee
The artist's minimum guaranteed payment, regardless of ticket sales.
Projected bar spend
Your estimate of bar revenue for the night based on expected attendance and historical averages.
Bar minimum
The minimum bar commitment (if any). The higher of bar spend and bar minimum is used in the calculation.
Labor forecast
Estimated total labor cost for the night (security, bar staff, door, etc.).
Production costs
Sound, lighting, backline, staging.
Facility costs
Cleaning, utilities, equipment wear.
Other costs
Anything else that isn't covered by the above categories.

Click Calculate — all math is done server-side. Never trust a browser total for booking decisions.

Results

Gross Revenue
(attendance × ticket price) + (attendance × fee) + rental + max(bar spend, bar minimum)
Estimated Cost
guarantee + labor + production + facility + other
Venue Net
Gross Revenue − Estimated Cost
Margin %
(Venue Net / Gross Revenue) × 100
Break-even tickets
⌈Estimated Cost / Ticket Price⌉ — must sell this many to cover fixed costs
Minimum guarantee tickets
⌈Guarantee / Ticket Price⌉ — tickets needed just to cover the artist

Risk flags

After calculating, the evaluator shows color-coded risk badges on the results panel:

FlagSeverityWhat it means
Negative marginRedCosts exceed revenue at projected attendance — this show loses money as modeled
Venue net negative with guaranteeRedThe venue is upside-down — the guarantee alone exceeds the projected net
Low margin under 15%YellowPositive but thin — any shortfall in attendance or bar will push it negative
Attendance below break-evenYellowThe projected headcount doesn't cover fixed costs — you're counting on selling more than expected
Bar spend below minimumYellowThe bar minimum kicks in — use the minimum figure, not the projection, in your net calculation
Projected attendance exceeds capacityOrangeThe attendance assumption exceeds the room's legal limit — not a valid scenario
Model your scenarios

Run the evaluator at multiple attendance levels: your best case, your realistic case, and your floor case (70% of expected). A deal that only works at full capacity is riskier than one that works at 70%. The evaluator saves your last calculation — you can adjust inputs and recalculate as many times as you need.

Converting a lead to an event

When a lead is in approved status (or evaluating if the deal is ready to go), click Convert to Event.

Backstage creates the event in a single transaction:

  • A new event record is created using the lead's details (title, event type, venue, expected date)
  • The lead is linked to the event with a permanent reference
  • Audit notes are written on both the lead and the new event
  • You're taken directly to the new event workspace to continue setup

After conversion, the lead is marked converted and becomes read-only. Its evaluation data — all projections, risk flags, and the calculation results — is preserved permanently on the lead record for audit and historical comparison against the actual settled numbers.

The lead lives on

Converting a lead does not delete it. A venue admin can always navigate back to the original lead from the event workspace to see what the deal looked like at evaluation time — useful for post-show retrospectives comparing projected vs. actual margin.

Chapter 2

Booking a Show

The booking process is a pipeline. Every event enters at the bottom (Hold) and works its way up. The system enforces what you need at each step — no contract, no Booked status.

How inquiries arrive

Inquiries come in by email, phone, web form, and sometimes someone just walking in the door. Regardless of how it arrives, the first thing you do is create an event in Backstage. Don't wait for details to be confirmed. Getting it in the system — even as a stub — is what holds the date.

The calendar color-codes by room:

  • Downstairs — orange tones
  • Upstairs — blue tones
  • Additional spaces — green tones
⚠️ Holds are not promises

Putting an event on Hold blocks the date visually on the calendar, but it does not commit the venue. A Hold means "this date is being considered." It becomes a firm commitment at Booked — which requires a signed contract.

Creating an event

Click the + New event button in the top-right corner of the screen. This opens the Event Creation Wizard — a 7-step guided form that collects everything needed for a new show: event basics, deal structure, contact details, financial terms, production requirements, and promotion settings. When you finish, the wizard creates both the event and a pre-populated contract draft in one click. This is the recommended path for any show that will have a booking agreement.

If you need a quick placeholder first and want to deal with the contract separately, click ⚡ Quick Create instead in the wizard's sidebar. A compact modal appears: pick a template (or Blank event), confirm date, title, and door/show times, and click Create event. The new event opens immediately in its workspace. Any date already entered in the wizard carries over to the modal.

The most important decision you make at creation time is Event Type. This changes how the event behaves throughout the system:

🎵 Public Show

Live music, DJ night, comedy, open mic, karaoke, etc. Has a full pipeline including promotion, ticketing, and a public event page. Uses Producer/Artist and Booker contacts.

🔒 Private Event / Rental

Corporate parties, birthday rentals, film shoots, album release parties, etc. Never publicly listed. Uses a Client contact. Has a shorter pipeline — no promotion stages.

Once you set the type to Private Event, both the wizard and the Quick Create modal change layout to show client fields instead of booker/artist fields. For detailed private event instructions, see Private Event Rentals later in this chapter.

⏰ Time auto-fill

If you set Doors, the system will automatically suggest Show time (1 hour after doors) and End time (5 hours after doors) if you leave those blank. You can always override. This saves time on common show formats.

Multi-day events

Most shows are a single night. Some aren't — a two-day festival, a weekend private rental, a multi-day workshop or artist residency. For those, set an End Date alongside the regular Date and the event spans every calendar day from Date through End Date instead of just one.

You can set End Date in the wizard's Event Basics step, in the Quick Create modal, or later from the event's own Details tab. Leave it blank (the default) for an ordinary single-day show — the field only matters when it's filled in.

📅 How it looks on the calendar

Instead of a single day's chip, the event renders as one continuous chip spanning every day in its range, on both the monthly calendar and the agenda view.

🚪 Room conflicts

The room is considered booked for the entire range. Another booking that overlaps any day inside that span — not just the first day — will be blocked as a conflict.

Everything else about the event — contract, staffing, ticketing, guest list, settlement — stays attached to that single event record exactly as it would for a normal show. A multi-day event is still one event; it just occupies a wider footprint on the calendar.

💡 Not the same thing as a recurring event

Multi-day is one event spanning several consecutive days (a festival). A recurring event is several separate events on a repeating schedule (a weekly karaoke night) — each with its own date, contract, and staffing. See the next section.

Recurring events

A recurring series is not a single event that magically reappears on multiple dates. Every occurrence is created as its own fully independent event — its own contract, staffing, ticketing, guest list, and status ladder. Occurrences are linked only so the app can show you the rest of the series and let you jump between them. Editing, cancelling, or rebooking one occurrence never touches any of the others.

Starting a series

There are two ways to spin one up:

  • From the event wizard — check Recurring Event on Step 1, Event Basics. The pattern is applied right after the first event is created, so the whole series exists by the time you finish the wizard.
  • From an existing event — open its Details tab and use the Recurrence panel underneath it. This turns that single event into the first occurrence of a brand-new series. (An event that's already part of a series can't be turned into the start of a second one.)

Choosing a pattern

The weekday (for weekly patterns) or day-of-month (for monthly patterns) is always taken from the date you're starting from — nobody picks it separately, so the pattern can never disagree with the date it was built from.

Recurrence picker
Weekly — every other week — on Tuesday
After 12 occurrences

Supported patterns:

  • Weekly — every week, every other week, every 3rd week, or every 4th week, on whichever day of the week the starting date falls on (e.g. "Every other Tuesday").
  • Monthly by weekday — the same ordinal weekday every month (e.g. "First Thursday of the month", or "Last Friday of the month").
  • Monthly by date — the same day-of-month every month (e.g. "the 15th").

An end condition is required — either after N occurrences or on a specific date — and the total is capped at 52 occurrences per series. A live preview lists the dates that will be created so staff can double-check before committing.

⚠️ Conflicts block the whole batch

Every generated date is checked against existing bookings at that venue, exactly like a normal event create. If any date in the pattern conflicts with an existing booking, nothing is created — you'll see an error listing which date(s) collided so you can adjust the pattern (a different weekday, a shorter range, fewer occurrences) and try again.

Managing an existing series

Open any occurrence's Details tab — the Recurrence panel lists every sibling in the series with its date and status, and links to jump straight to any of them. If one occurrence needs to drop out (it was cancelled, moved to a different venue, whatever), use Remove this event from the series on that occurrence. It becomes a fully standalone event again; the rest of the series is unaffected.

💡 No bulk edit across a series

There's no "change this and all future occurrences" action. Each occurrence is a completely normal event once created — update its time, venue, staffing, contract, or anything else the same way you would for any other show, one at a time.

📞 Producer/Artist vs. Booker

The Producer/Artist is the person putting on the show — the band, the DJ, the comedy promoter. The Booker is the person at the venue (or agent) who arranged the deal. These can be the same person. Both are required at Hold because the automatic email notifications need them.

Once the Hold is saved, the event appears on the calendar with a colored chip showing the event name and door time. Other staff can see the date is taken.

Step 2: Intake Complete

Intake Complete means all the financial and logistical details are confirmed and you're ready to build the contract. Think of it as the "everything we need to write the deal" checkpoint.

To advance from Hold to Intake Complete, you must have the following on top of the Hold requirements:

Required for Intake Complete

The dollar amount of the deposit agreed to. This will appear in the contract.
Ticket price
The advance ticket price for the show. Required so the contract and ticketing setup can reference it.
Capacity
The hard maximum attendance. This is a venue and safety constraint — not an estimate.
Age restriction
e.g. "All Ages", "18+", "21+" — affects door operations and contract language.

When the status changes to Intake Complete, the system automatically emails venue admins with a notification so they can begin contract drafting.

💡 Load-in time

While not strictly required for Intake Complete, this is a good time to confirm and enter the Load-In / Tech time. The band's crew and sound tech need to know their call time, and it shows on the calendar chip so operations staff can see it at a glance.

Step 3: Booked

Booked is the biggest gate in the pipeline. An event cannot reach Booked status without a contract on file. The system checks the Contracts tab — if there's no contract in approved, sent, or signed status, the status change will be blocked.

When a show reaches Booked:

  • The date is locked — the system treats this as a firm commitment
  • All venue admins are notified
  • The event moves from the "pending" part of the pipeline to the "active show" section
  • The next step is getting promotional assets from the producer

The contract workflow

Contracts live in the Contracts tab inside the event workspace. The system has a built-in deal builder — you don't write the contract from scratch, you fill in the terms and the system assembles the document.

Starting a contract

In the Contracts tab, click + New contract. You'll be prompted to choose a template. For most shows, you'll use one of these:

TemplateWhen to use
Promoter / Production ShowExternal promoter producing the event. Split-revenue or flat-rental deal.
Artist / Band PerformanceDirect booking. Band plays for a guarantee and/or door split.
Famous / High-Draw ArtistHeadline shows with larger guarantees, radius clauses, and hospitality riders.
Private Event RentalVenue rental — corporate, personal, or private event.
Recurring Night AgreementMonthly or weekly residency. Has recurrence, review, and termination terms.

After choosing a template, the system will pre-populate smart clauses based on the deal terms. For example: if the show is "All Ages," the underage admission clause is automatically included. If there's a bar minimum, the bar minimum clause appears. You can add or remove clauses from the list.

The contract status ladder

Contracts move through two phases. First, the draft phase (internal review and approval), then the e-sign phase (sent out, signed by all parties, and fully executed). Each phase has its own status progression:

Draft phase

Draft → Needs Review → Approved → Ready to Send

E-sign phase

Sent → Viewed → Partially Signed → Signed by Client → Countersigned → Fully Executed ✓
Terminal states (can occur at any point): Voided Declined Expired Cancelled Superseded

The event can be moved to Booked once the contract reaches Approved status or better. When a contract reaches Fully Executed, the event is automatically advanced to Booked.

⚠️ Unfilled contract terms

Before you can mark a contract as Sent or Signed, every required field in the included clauses must be filled. The system will show you a list of what's still missing. Common ones: marketing_deadline, insurance_amount, and the guarantee amount if there's a guarantee clause.

The counterparty

Every contract has two parties: the venue and the counterparty — whoever is on the other side of the deal.

ScenarioCounterparty is…
Band / artist bookingThe artist, band, or their booking agent
Promoter dealThe external promoter or event organizer
Private / venue rentalThe client or their organization

Set the counterparty in the contract editor using the Counterparty Name, Organization, and Email fields. The name and organization combine on the printed contract — e.g., "Chris Robison (The Panic Band)". The email address is used when you send the contract.

📋 Private events auto-fill the counterparty

For private events, the counterparty name and organization are pulled from the Client / Primary Contact fields on the event. You don't need to enter them again — just confirm they're correct when you open the contract editor.

Downloading and printing a contract

Once the contract has content, two export buttons appear at the top of the contract editor:

Electronic signatures

Panic Backstage has a built-in electronic signature system — no DocuSign or third-party account required. Once a contract is approved, you send it for signature with a single click. Each signer receives a secure, personalised email with a time-limited signing link. When all parties have signed, the system generates a tamper-evident Final Executed PDF — the legally authoritative copy — and automatically advances the linked event to Booked.

📋 No account needed for signers

The counterparty and other external signers do not need a Backstage account. They sign directly through a public link (/sign.html?token=…). The link is personalised, single-use, and expires after the configured time window (default 7 days).

Sending a contract for e-signature

  1. Open the contract in the builder. It must be in Approved or Ready to Send status and have no missing required terms.
  2. Click Send for Signature in the right-hand panel. A confirmation dialog appears showing the signer list — name and email address for each party.
  3. The counterparty's details are pre-filled from the contract's Counterparty fields. Add, remove, or edit signers as needed. You can include extra signers such as a guarantor or an artist representative.
  4. Click Confirm & Send. Each signer receives a personalised Please sign: [Contract Title] email with a Review & Sign button.
  5. The contract status advances to Sent. The Signers panel in the right rail shows each signer's live status: Pending, Viewed, Signed, or Declined.
Signer statusMeaning
PendingEmail sent; signer has not yet opened their link
ViewedSigner opened the link and saw the contract

DeclinedSigner clicked Decline (a note may be attached)
⚠️ Missing required terms block sending

The Send for Signature button is disabled if any required clause variable is still blank. Use the Review panel to find and fill what's missing before sending.

What the signer sees

When a signer clicks the Review & Sign button in their email they land on a clean, mobile-friendly signing page. Here is what happens:

  1. The full contract text is displayed for review.
  2. At the bottom, two signing methods are offered side by side:
    • Type Signature — the signer types their name; it is rendered in a cursive font as a legal signature.
    • Draw Signature — a canvas lets them draw their signature with a mouse or finger.
  3. The signer checks the consent box: "I agree to use electronic records and signatures for this agreement…"
  4. The Sign Agreement button becomes active once a method is chosen and consent is given. Clicking it records the signature.
  5. A confirmation screen thanks the signer and notes when the fully-executed copy will be emailed to them.

If the signer decides not to sign, they can click Decline at any time. A short dialog lets them leave an optional note explaining why. You will be notified by email and the contract status will reflect the decline.

📱 Works on any device

The signing page is optimised for phones and tablets. The draw canvas is touch-enabled and renders at full device pixel ratio for crisp signature images on retina screens.

Resending, voiding, and countersigning

These actions are available in the Signers panel on the right side of the contract editor, visible once the contract has been sent.

ActionWhen to useEffect
Resend link Signer says their link expired or they deleted the email Generates a fresh signing link for that signer only and re-sends the email. The old link is immediately invalidated.
Void contract Terms changed significantly; deal fell through; wrong contract sent Cancels the signing process, invalidates all outstanding links, and records a reason. Status becomes Voided. You must start a new contract to re-engage.
Countersign All external signers have signed (status = Signed by Client) Admin provides their own typed or drawn signature to countersign on behalf of the venue. Triggers final PDF generation and event advancement.
⚠️ Void is permanent

Voiding a contract cannot be undone. All outstanding signing links become invalid immediately, and any signer who tries their link will see a message that it is no longer valid. If you need to fix terms and re-send, create a new contract (you can copy the deal terms) and send that one instead.

📋 Append-only

The audit log is append-only at the database level — no entry can ever be edited or deleted. It is reproduced in full on the last page of the Final Executed PDF as the audit certificate, giving both parties a permanent, self-contained record of every step in the signing lifecycle.

The Final Executed PDF

When a contract reaches Fully Executed status, Backstage generates a single locked PDF that becomes the authoritative contract record. It contains:

  • The full contract body exactly as it was approved — no edits are possible after the first signer signs.
  • A signature block for each signer showing:
    • Their typed or drawn signature (reproduced at full quality)
    • Full name, title (if provided), and company
    • Date, time, and IP address of signing
  • A final Audit Certificate page listing every lifecycle event from contract creation through full execution.
  • A document hash — the SHA-256 fingerprint of the PDF bytes, stored in the database so the file's integrity can be verified at any time.

To download the final PDF, open the contract and click ⬇ Download Final PDF in the right rail. This button only appears on contracts in Fully Executed status.

💡 Verifying the document hasn't changed

The SHA-256 hash shown in the audit log and stored in the database lets you confirm the downloaded PDF is the original, unmodified copy. If the file's hash matches the database record, it hasn't been tampered with. If they differ, treat the file as suspect and contact Backstage support.

📋 Email notification when fully executed

When a contract is fully executed, each signer automatically receives a Your agreement is signed email with a link to download their copy of the final PDF. Venue admins receive a separate notification confirming the execution.

Chapter 4

Post-Event

The show is over. Now you count the money, pay the bands, and close the books. Here's how the system handles the end of an event's life.

The show ends — auto-archive

You don't need to manually close out shows. Every morning, the system checks whether any active events have a date that's already passed. If an event is in any active status (Booked, Published, etc.) and its date is in the past, the system automatically moves it to Archived.

When an event is auto-archived:

  • The status changes to Archived
  • The settlement team receives an email reminder to file settlement
  • The event moves to the "past shows" section of the pipeline board
  • The public event page is hidden (if it was live)
📅 When does it happen?

The auto-archive cron runs every morning. So if your show was Saturday night, expect the event to be archived by Sunday morning. If an event date passes and the show was canceled, it will also be archived — so check the status after archiving if you need to record a cancellation instead.

Settlement — the big picture

Settlement is the financial close-out of a show. You're reconciling what came in (ticket sales, bar) against what went out (expenses, band payments, promoter payout) to arrive at what the venue actually made.

The settlement process has two parts:

  1. The night-of settlement doc — the physical or digital tally sheet done at the end of the show, often a Google Sheet or a handwritten form. The person running money tracks sales and expenses in real time during the event.
  2. Entering it in Backstage — once the night-of doc is finalized, those numbers get entered into the Settlement tab so the system has a record.
🔗 Link your settlement doc

In the Settlement tab, there's a field for Settlement document — a URL or note pointing to your night-of tally (e.g., a Google Sheets link). Paste it here. This connects Backstage to the source document and also syncs the URL back to the connected tracking spreadsheet.

Closeout overview

After the event ends and it's been archived, the Closeout tab in the event workspace becomes the primary workspace. It replaces the old Settlement form with a structured financial ledger workflow.

The Closeout panel has two columns:

Events Awaiting Closeout

To correct a mistake: void the incorrect entry (it stays visible with strikethrough so the correction is traceable) and add a new corrected entry. This ensures a complete audit trail — every number that was ever entered is visible.

The closeout checklist

Before the ledger can be finalized, seven items must be checked off. Each checkbox updates the server immediately when clicked — no save button needed.

7-Point Closeout Checklist
  • Contract Signed — a fully executed contract is on file for this event
  • Deposit Received — the deposit was received (or waived)
  • Vendors Confirmed — all on-site vendors have confirmed day-of attendance
  • Staffing Confirmed — all scheduled shifts are in Confirmed or Completed status
  • Bar Closed — bar has been closed out and receipts collected
  • Cash Reconciled — any cash sales or payments have been counted and reconciled
  • All Invoices Collected — outstanding invoices from vendors have been received

The Finalize button remains disabled until all seven items are checked. This is enforced server-side — checking boxes in the UI does not unlock finalization if the server-side state disagrees.

Checking items requires manage_ledger

Checking off closeout checklist items requires the manage_ledger capability. Venue admins have this by default. If a staff member is helping with closeout and cannot check items, their account needs this capability added.

Finalize & reopen

Finalizing the closeout

Once all seven checklist items are checked, click Finalize. Finalizing:

  • Locks the ledger — no new entries can be added and no existing entries can be voided
  • Sets the event status to settled
  • Records the finalizer's name, timestamp, and the final P&L snapshot
  • Removes the event from the "Events Awaiting Closeout" dashboard card

Finalizing requires the finalize_closeout capability (venue admin by default).

Finalization locks the books

Once finalized, the ledger is read-only. Reopening is possible but audited and requires a mandatory reason. Don't finalize until the numbers are right — verify the P&L summary against your night-of records before clicking Finalize.

Reopening a finalized closeout

If something was missed after finalization — a vendor invoice arrived late, a damage charge needs to be added — click Reopen. Reopening requires:

  • The finalize_closeout capability (venue admin)
  • A mandatory reason — it cannot be left blank

The reason and timestamp are permanently recorded in the audit trail. After reopening, the ledger is editable again and the event falls back onto the "Events Awaiting Closeout" dashboard. When corrections are complete, finalize again.

Reopen is audited forever

Every reopen — who did it, when, and why — is permanently stored. This prevents "just fix the numbers quietly" situations and gives your accounting team a clear record of any post-finalization adjustments.

In-House Ticketing

The venue owns the ticket, the token, and the audit trail. Buyers pay through Stripe or Square's hosted page — card data never touches Backstage. Everything else — inventory, QR codes, door scanning, comps, and refunds — lives here.

External vs. in-house

Every event starts in External ticketing mode — meaning if someone clicks "Buy Tickets" on the public event page, they're sent to whatever URL you specify (TIXR, Eventbrite, a direct Stripe link, etc.).

When you switch to In-house mode, the system takes over: it shows a live ticket purchase form directly on the event page, handles payment, sends QR-code tickets by email, and enables the door scanner.

To switch modes, open the event workspace → Ticketing tab → choose the mode and click Save ticketing mode.

External mode

Enter a ticket page URL and the platform name. That's it. The public page shows a "Buy Tickets" button pointing to that URL. All sales, refunds, and reporting are handled by the external platform.

In-house mode

Full Backstage ticketing. You control the inventory, pricing, comps, scanner links, and reporting. Requires Stripe or Square to be configured in Admin → Payments.

🚀 First time switching to in-house

The first time you switch an event to in-house mode, the system automatically seeds a default ticket type (General Admission), a small comp allocation, and a Door scanner link — so you're not starting from zero. Edit or delete these defaults as needed.

How a ticket purchase works

Understanding what happens when a buyer purchases a ticket helps you troubleshoot issues and answer customer questions:

  1. Buyer opens the public event page and sees the in-house purchase form with available tiers and live inventory.
  2. Buyer clicks "Buy" — Backstage creates a pending order with a 15-minute hold on the inventory. This prevents two buyers from buying the last ticket simultaneously.
  3. Buyer is redirected to the payment provider's hosted page (Stripe Checkout or Square) to enter card details. Card data never touches Backstage.
  4. After payment, the provider sends a webhook to Backstage. Backstage verifies the signature, then issues the tickets and sends each holder a QR code email.
  5. The 15-minute hold on inventory is released and replaced by the actual sold quantity.
⏱️ The 15-minute hold window

If a buyer starts checkout but doesn't complete payment, their hold on the inventory expires after 15 minutes and the tickets become available again. They'll need to start over if they return after the window. This means your "Available" count might temporarily look lower than it actually is during busy sale periods.

venue_admin Full-time management staff who need to do everything — venue managers, ownership
global_viewer Read-only observers across all events — accountants, silent investors, absentee owners
event_owner The person responsible for a specific show — same power as venue_admin for their own event
promoter External promoters running a show — can manage lineup, schedule, guest list; can't touch money or contracts
band / artist Performers or their managers — upload promo assets and see their assigned tasks only
designer Graphic designers — upload and manage assets only; no access to operational details
staff Floor managers, production coordinators — run sheet + guest list + tasks; no financial access
viewer Read-only on events they're specifically invited to — the default for new accounts with limited access
